Scoring guide — three separate inputs

Each risk requires three separate scores. These are explained in full in Topic 8.3 on the Week 8 CloudDeakin page, including the colour-coded matrix and a worked example. Review that page before completing this activity.

  • Likelihood (1–5) — how probable is this risk in the next 12 months?
    1 = rare   2 = unlikely   3 = possible   4 = likely   5 = almost certain
  • Consequence (1–5) — how serious would the impact be if it occurred?
    1 = negligible   2 = minor   3 = moderate   4 = major   5 = catastrophic
  • Risk score — calculated automatically as Likelihood × Consequence.
    1–5 = Low (green)   6–11 = Moderate (amber)   12–19 = High (orange)   20–25 = Extreme (red)

3. Reflection

Quality check

Have you covered all five categories with at least one risk each?

Are all risk descriptions specific to your practice business — not generic phrases that could apply to any business?

Have you scored likelihood and consequence independently — not just assigned a combined number that feels right?

Are your mitigation actions for the three highest-scoring risks genuinely implementable in the next four weeks?

Did you resist scoring everything low? A register dominated by green rows is almost certainly optimism bias.

Why eight minimum?

A micro-firm doing a genuine first-pass risk assessment will typically identify 8–12 meaningful risks. Five risks across five categories means some categories will be skipped and the most obvious risks crowd out the important ones. Eight rows with mandatory category coverage forces the breadth that real risk management requires.

Before you export

  • All five categories covered.
  • At least eight rows fully complete.
  • Risk descriptions are specific — not generic.
  • Likelihood and consequence scored independently.
  • Mitigation actions written for three highest-scoring risks.
  • Reflection identifies what the scoring process revealed.
